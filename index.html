Final Thoughts How I Completed TryHackMe Advent of Cyber 2025: 24 Days of Hands-On Cybersecurity Learning By Niketan Gosavi | Aspiring SOC Analyst | TryHackMe Top 6% | @Darkhunter2205 "The best way to learn cybersecurity is to actually do it." Cybersecurity is one of those fields where reading alone isn't enough. You can watch videos, read blogs, and complete courses, but sooner or later you have to get your hands dirty and solve real problems. That's exactly why I participated in TryHackMe's Advent of Cyber 2025. For 24 consecutive days, I worked through challenges covering blue team operations, malware analysis, phishing, web security, AI security, incident response, and much more. By the end of the event, I had gained practical experience across multiple cybersecurity domains while strengthening the skills that are directly relevant to my goal of becoming a SOC Analyst. This article is a summary of my experience, the skills I learned, and why I believe every cybersecurity learner should participate in Advent of Cyber. What Is Advent of Cyber? Every December, TryHackMe hosts Advent of Cyber, one of the largest free cybersecurity learning events in the world. Instead of opening a traditional advent calendar, participants unlock a new cybersecurity challenge every day from December 1st through December 24th. Each challenge focuses on a different cybersecurity topic and combines learning material with practical exercises. Whether you're interested in offensive security, defensive security, cloud security, malware analysis, digital forensics, or threat hunting, there is something for everyone. The 2025 edition followed a story-driven adventure set in the town of Wareville, where participants solved incidents and challenges while helping defend the town against various cyber threats. What makes Advent of Cyber unique is that it doesn't feel like a traditional course. Every day introduces a completely different challenge, forcing participants to constantly adapt and learn new concepts. Why I Decided to Join Before Advent of Cyber 2025, I was already spending a significant amount of time learning cybersecurity through TryHackMe, Hack The Box, and various certification programs. However, I wanted something that would challenge me consistently every day. Many learners, including myself, sometimes focus heavily on a single area. One month might be web exploitation. Another month might be malware analysis. While specialization is important, real-world cybersecurity requires exposure to many different domains. When I saw the range of topics covered in Advent of Cyber, I knew it was exactly the kind of challenge I needed. I made a simple commitment to myself: Complete every challenge and learn something new every day. The Prep Track Before Day 1 officially started, TryHackMe released a Prep Track designed to help participants get ready. The Prep Track covered: • Linux fundamentals • Password security • Cyber hygiene concepts • Virtual machine access • Platform navigation Although I already had experience with most of these topics, it was a useful refresher and helped me get into the learning mindset before the main event began. For beginners, this preparation track is extremely valuable because it removes many of the technical barriers that often discourage people from getting started. What I Learned Across 24 Days One of the things I appreciated most about Advent of Cyber 2025 was the variety. Every day introduced a different challenge and often a completely different mindset. Some days focused on offensive security, while others focused on defensive operations, investigations, and incident response. Topics included: • Linux fundamentals • Web application security • Phishing attacks • Splunk and SIEM analysis • Malware analysis • IDOR vulnerabilities • Network discovery with Nmap • AI prompt injection attacks • Password cracking with John the Ripper • SOC alert triaging using Microsoft Sentinel • OSINT investigations • Cloud security concepts • Digital forensics • Cryptography challenges This diversity forced me to move outside my comfort zone and learn skills that I may not have explored otherwise. My Favourite Challenges Malware Analysis One of my favourite challenges involved malware analysis. The challenge required investigating a suspicious file using both static and dynamic analysis techniques. Using tools such as Pestudio and Regshot, I examined the file's behavior and observed how it interacted with the operating system. What made this challenge valuable was how closely it reflected real-world security operations. As a SOC analyst, understanding how malware behaves and how to identify malicious indicators is a critical skill. This challenge felt less like a CTF and more like a practical security investigation. SOC Alert Triaging Another standout challenge focused on alert triaging inside Microsoft Sentinel. This was especially interesting because it closely aligned with my long-term goal of working in a Security Operations Center. The challenge involved: • Investigating alerts • Reviewing evidence • Understanding attack indicators • Determining whether activity was malicious • Closing or escalating incidents For me, this felt like a realistic simulation of the daily responsibilities of a SOC analyst. Instead of simply finding a flag, I had to think critically and investigate what was actually happening. Phishing Analysis The phishing challenge provided insight into how social engineering attacks are created and executed. Understanding phishing from the attacker's perspective helps defenders recognize suspicious behavior and identify indicators of compromise more effectively. Since phishing remains one of the most common attack vectors used by threat actors, this challenge reinforced the importance of security awareness and email investigation skills. How Advent of Cyber Improved My SOC Skills As someone pursuing a career in Security Operations, this was probably the most valuable aspect of the event. Many people think cybersecurity is all about hacking systems. What Advent of Cyber reinforced is that defending systems is equally important. Several challenges required skills that SOC analysts use every day: • Log analysis • Alert investigation • Incident triage • Threat detection • Malware analysis • OSINT investigations • Understanding attacker techniques More importantly, the event helped me develop an investigation mindset. The tools changed every day, but the questions remained the same: What happened? How do I prove it? What evidence supports my conclusion? Learning to answer those questions is one of the most important skills for anyone pursuing a SOC role. The Badges I Earned Completing Advent of Cyber 2025 earned me several badges on TryHackMe, including: 🔥 30 Day Streak 🎄 Advent of Cyber 2025 🏆 Five Scenarios Completed 🎯 100% True Positive Rate 🔔 First Alert Closed 🎯 First Scenario Completed 🎰 Raffle Royalty – Hack2Win 2025 Participant Among these, the Five Scenarios Completed badge is one that I'm particularly proud of because it represents completing practical investigation scenarios rather than simply solving isolated challenges. Lessons I Took Away After completing all 24 days, here are the biggest lessons I learned. 1. Cybersecurity Is a Huge Field No single person can know everything. One day you're investigating malware. The next day you're analyzing logs. The next day you're learning about AI security. The field constantly evolves, and continuous learning is essential. 2. Tools Are Not the Goal Anyone can learn commands. The real skill is understanding why you're using a tool and how to interpret the results. A skilled analyst doesn't just run Nmap. A skilled analyst understands what the scan reveals and what actions should follow. 3. Consistency Beats Motivation Completing a challenge every day may not seem significant at first. But after 24 days, those small daily efforts compound into meaningful progress. The biggest lesson wasn't technical. It was learning the value of consistency. 4. Blue Team Skills Deserve More Attention Many newcomers are attracted to offensive security. However, some of the most valuable challenges involved incident response, alert triaging, and threat investigation. Those skills are directly applicable to real-world security operations.
